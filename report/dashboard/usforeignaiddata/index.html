Follow the money to its purpose

The funnel: promised → delivered → purpose

Three gates. First: how much of what was promised actually arrived. Second: of the money that arrived, how much went to the stated purpose — health, education, agriculture — and how much to running the machine. Mature accounts deliver 84–100% of what they promise. But of every delivered dollar, about 18 cents went to explicitly administrative and support lines — and that is only the visible overhead; what partners spend on their own administration inside program awards is not published anywhere.

Cumulative FY2015–FY2026. Rates above 100% mean disbursements drew on promises made before FY2015. MCC's low rate is timing, not failure: the compact disburses until August 2028.
"Program Support" (red) bundles operating expenses, administration and oversight, and program design/evaluation — as classified by the US government itself.
People vs the system

How much reached people, and how much ran the machinery

Every delivered dollar, sorted three ways using the US government's own sector labels: money whose immediate object is a person (a meal, a clinic visit, a classroom, a water tap, a farmer's training) — money that builds the systems around people (courts, grids, roads, ministries, markets, disease surveillance) — and money that runs the aid machine itself. The classification is ours, it is published in full below, and reasonable people can move lines: building a health system is not waste, it is how care continues after the project ends.

Honesty notes: "machine" counts only explicitly booked administration (Direct Administrative Costs, M&E, operating expenses). The overhead partners charge inside program awards is not published anywhere, so the true cost of the system is higher than 18%. And "system" money can serve people deeply — a maternal clinic needs a grid and a road; the split shows emphasis, not virtue.
The plumbing

Which budget accounts carry the money

US aid is not one pot: it flows through named budget accounts created by Congress. For Nepal the backbone has been the Economic Support Fund, Global Health, and Development Assistance. Watch the orange MCC compact arrive in FY2025–26 as most other accounts fade.

Where it landed

Beneath the primes: the money's onward journey

Big international partners win the awards, but much of the money is sub-granted onward. Across the 154 largest projects, $1.06 billion — about half of what was obligated — was passed to 562 other organisations, many of them Nepali firms and local NGOs, reaching 48 of Nepal's 77 districts.

Accountability

What the auditors found

The US Inspector General audits US assistance to Nepal. Across the seven Nepal audits we could trace, total questioned costs were $323,161 — about 0.016% of the $2.0bn delivered, and mostly small ineligible or unsupported items on funds managed directly by the Government of Nepal. We archived four of the audit PDFs here with checksums; the rest now live only on USAID's offline document server.

Method

Source and integrity

All figures come from the official ForeignAssistance.gov data service (the same one behind the US government's Nepal country page), retrieved 2026-06-04, using three independent cuts of the same money: by sector, by funding account, and by implementing agency. The three cuts reconcile to the dollar in every year. Only actual flows are shown (obligations and disbursements); budget requests and appropriations are excluded. Constant dollars are as published, base year 2024. Account and program names are kept in English because they are official US budget titles.
